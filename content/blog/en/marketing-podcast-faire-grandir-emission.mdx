There's a stubborn belief among podcasters: "if the show is good, it'll grow eventually." It's false, and it's what keeps hundreds of good podcasts stuck. The bottleneck is almost never the quality of the episode. It's **discovery**. Your next listener isn't searching for your show. It has to land in front of them.

## Why audio alone no longer cuts it

An audio file doesn't travel. It sits in a listening app, waiting for someone to come find it. Vertical video, on the other hand, gets pushed to strangers by the recommendation engine. That's the entire gap between content people have to find and content that comes to them.

By turning every episode into clips, you convert an hour of conversation into dozens of entry points across TikTok, Shorts and Reels. Every clip that works is free advertising for the show, with a name, a voice and a face that lodge in someone's memory. That's exactly the mechanism that generated +23.1M views for Kyan Khojandi's podcast.

## The mistake that kills it: not recording for the clip

Here's the truth few agencies will tell you before you sign: **not every podcast is clippable.** If your guests are reading their notes, if the framing is loose, if the strong moments stretch across ten minutes, no editor will save the clip.

A podcast that grows fast is a podcast recorded to be cut: a decent camera, eye contact with the lens at moments, and above all **punchy stretches you actually commit to**, a sharp take every few minutes rather than a smooth, even conversation. Think "clip" while you're recording, not just while you're editing.

## The 3 clips that turn a stranger into a listener

1. **The opinion clip.** A guest takes a stance, picks a side, ruffles a few feathers. It sparks debate in the comments, and the comments feed the reach. It's the format that travels furthest.
2. **The advice clip.** One actionable idea in 30 seconds. People save it and follow for more. It's the format that converts best into subscribers.
3. **The emotion clip.** A burst of laughter, a confession, a sincere moment. It doesn't always pull the most views, but it's what builds attachment to the show, and therefore loyalty.

## Consistency beats perfection

A podcast that ships one "perfect" clip a week grows slower than a podcast that shows up every day with clips that are simply good enough. The algorithm rewards consistency, and the human ear needs repetition to remember a voice. Cadence isn't an execution detail: it's the strategy.
